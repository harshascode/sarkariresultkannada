**Pendulum Review – A Mind‑Bending Thriller That Turns Dreams Inside‑Out**



## Movie Summary & Story  

*Pendulum* is a Telugu‑dubbed remake of Rejin S. Babu’s Malayalam original, and it daringly drags the audience into a maze of lucid dreams, time loops and existential riddles. The film follows Dr. Mahesh Narayan (Vijay Babu), a respected neurologist who has just returned to Kerala from Australia with his wife Shwetha (Devaki Rajendran) and their young daughter Thanmayi. A seemingly ordinary family outing to the mist‑clad hills of Marayur turns sinister when Mahesh experiences an unsettling déjà vu, is plagued by fragmented nightmares, and later wakes up unconscious on a deserted road after a truck collision.

The narrative splits into two parallel threads. One thread tracks Mahesh’s frantic attempts to make sense of his altered perception, aided by his old friend Dr. Jain Joseph and a lucid‑dream guru called John Master. The other thread introduces two children, Amir and Angel, who share a mysterious dream‑link that appears to echo Mahesh’s own experiences. As the story unfolds, the film suggests that Mahesh’s consciousness is somehow entangled with the children’s dreamscape, creating a feedback loop where past, present and future bleed into each other.

What raises the stakes is the implication that Mahesh’s research could unlock the secret to controlling time itself—an ability that, if misused, could rewrite personal histories and even alter the fate of those he loves. The film therefore becomes a race against an invisible clock: Mahesh must untangle the dream‑logic before the loop collapses, or risk being trapped forever in a liminal space where reality is a mirage. The tension is not merely about solving a mystery; it is about preserving one’s own identity and protecting the family that anchors him to the real world.

While the premise feels lofty, the screenplay grounds it with emotional beats—Mahesh’s growing desperation, Shwetha’s quiet resilience, and the children’s innocent yet eerie synchronicity. The story deliberately avoids the typical masala tropes of Telugu cinema, opting instead for a cerebral thriller that asks the audience to question the thin line between waking life and the subconscious.

## Technical Aspects  

**Direction** – Rejin S. Babu tackles an intricate script with surprising confidence. He leans on narrative ingenuity rather than spectacle, using subtle visual cues to differentiate dream layers. The pacing is deliberately uneven: a deliberate, almost meditative first act that establishes the characters, followed by a brisker second half where the lucid‑dream mechanics kick in. This shift keeps the audience engaged without overwhelming them with exposition.

**Cinematography** – Arun Damodaran’s work is the film’s visual backbone. Instead of relying on heavy CGI, he crafts atmosphere through chiaroscuro lighting and strategic framing. The hill‑top locales of Marayur are captured with a misty palette that feels both grounded and otherworldly, while the dream sequences employ shallow focus and slow‑motion inserts to create a sense of temporal distortion. The camera often mirrors Mahesh’s disorientation, using handheld shots that gradually transition to steadier compositions as he regains control.

**Editing** – Sooraj E S stitches the non‑linear narrative together with a rhythm that feels purposeful. The intercutting of Mahesh’s present with the children’s dream‑scenes is executed with clean match cuts, preventing the story from spiralling into confusion. The editor also knows when to linger—allowing a lingering shot of Mahesh’s bewildered stare to build tension—versus when to cut sharply, especially during the chase‑like sequences where the stakes heighten.

**Music & Background Score** – Composer Jean P Johnson supplies a haunting, synth‑laden score that underlines the film’s cerebral mood. Themes recur as leitmotifs: a low, pulsating bass for the “real world” and an ethereal choir for the dream realm. The three songs—“Mayum Kaalangal,” “Athirukal Marayave,” and “Roohin Nizhale”—are placed sparingly, each serving to deepen emotional resonance rather than provide a commercial break. Notably, a recurring piano motif signals transitions between timelines, helping viewers track the shifting reality.

**Production Design & Sound** – The production design stays minimalistic, using natural locations to reinforce authenticity. Ambient sounds—wind rustling through pine, distant temple bells—are amplified in the sound mix, heightening the immersive quality of the dream sequences.



## Performances & Characters  

**Vijay Babu (Dr. Mahesh Narayan)** – Babu delivers a nuanced portrait of a scientist caught between empirical logic and the irrationality of dreams. His restrained body language, especially the slight tremor in his hands during the lucid‑dream explanations, conveys an inner turmoil that feels genuine. He balances vulnerability with the steely determination required to confront an incomprehensible phenomenon.

**Devaki Rajendran (Shwetha)** – As Mahesh’s wife, Rajendran provides the emotional anchor. Her performance is understated but effective; she exudes calm strength, often acting as the voice of reason when Mahesh spirals into panic. The chemistry between the couple feels authentic, making the stakes of Mahesh’s quest feel personal rather than abstract.

**Anumol (Angel)** – Anumol’s mysterious aura adds a layer of intrigue. She inhabits her role with an almost spectral presence, allowing the audience to feel that she is both a victim and a conduit of the dream‑link. Her limited dialogue is delivered with a haunting poise that elevates the film’s eerie tone.

**Supporting Cast** – Ramesh Pisharody (Dr. Jain Joseph) and Prakash Bare (John Master) bring gravitas to the scientific and esoteric aspects, respectively. Indrans and Shobi Thilakan provide brief yet memorable moments of grounded humor, preventing the narrative from becoming overly clinical. The child actors, despite occasional over‑dramatic lines, manage to convey a sense of innocence that contrasts sharply with the film’s complex themes.

**Overall** – The ensemble works cohesively, each performance reinforcing the central theme of reality versus perception. The occasional stiffness in dialogue is offset by the actors’ commitment to their emotional beats.

## What Works & What Doesn’t  

**Pros**  
- **Innovative premise** – Lucid‑dreaming and time loops are rarely explored in Telugu cinema.  
- **Strong lead performance** – Vijay Babu anchors the film with conviction.  
- **Cinematography** – Atmospheric visuals that blend realism with surrealism.  
- **Score** – Jean P Johnson’s music accentuates tension without overpowering scenes.  
- **Narrative ambition** – Non‑linear storytelling keeps viewers intellectually engaged.

**Cons**  
- **Dialogue stiffness** – Some exposition feels lecture‑like, breaking immersion.  
- **Pacing in the first act** – The opening feels slow, risking early disengagement.  
- **Ambiguous ending** – Several plot threads remain unresolved, leaving the audience with unanswered questions.  
- **Limited VFX** – While purposeful, the minimal visual effects may disappoint viewers expecting high‑tech sci‑fi spectacles.



## My Rating & Final Verdict  

**Rating: 4 / 5**  

*Pendulum* is a breath of fresh air for anyone tired of formulaic action‑romance fare. It daringly pushes Telugu cinema toward a more cerebral direction, handling complex concepts like lucid dreaming and temporal loops with a blend of artistic restraint and narrative daring. The film’s technical execution—particularly its cinematography and score—punches above its budget, and Vijay Babu’s performance is a standout.

The drawbacks—uneven pacing, occasional wooden dialogue, and an ending that leans toward the cryptic—prevent it from achieving perfection. Nevertheless, the film’s willingness to experiment makes it a must‑watch for fans of mind‑bending thrillers.

**Verdict:** If you enjoy movies that make you think long after the credits roll and are open to a slower, puzzle‑like storytelling style, *Pendulum* is well worth your time. Stream it on ETV Win and prepare for a cinematic ride that blurs the line between waking life and the world of dreams.
