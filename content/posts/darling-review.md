## Darling Review – A Flirty Premise That Fizzles Out  



### 1. Movie Summary & Story  

*Darling* arrives as a Telugu romantic comedy that banks on a quirky “multiple‑personality” twist. The narrative follows Arjun (Priyadarshi), a good‑natured bachelor who, after a series of misadventures, ends up marrying Meera (Nabha Natesh). The novelty lies in Meera’s dissociative identity disorder, which causes her to oscillate between distinct personas—each with its own quirks, speech patterns, and emotional baggage.  

The film sets up its stakes early: Arjun must navigate the unpredictable shifts in his wife’s behaviour while trying to keep his own sanity intact. The story promises a blend of heartfelt moments—exploring acceptance, love beyond surface appearances—and slap‑stick comedy that capitalises on the chaos created by the personality switches.  

Unfortunately, the premise, while ripe for both humor and empathy, never quite reaches its full potential. The first half leans into the absurdity with a few genuinely funny set‑pieces, but the second half loses momentum, opting for drawn‑out melodrama rather than deepening the emotional core. The setting—a contemporary urban backdrop peppered with family gatherings and workplace scenes—offers a relatable canvas, yet the script rarely exploits it to underline the themes of identity and partnership. By the time the climax arrives, the audience is left with a plot that feels more like a series of sketches stitched together than a cohesive story arc.

### 3. Technical Aspects  

**Cinematography** – Naresh Ramadurai’s camera work is serviceable but never rises above the ordinary. The film relies on conventional framing, with bright lighting that suits the comedy tone, yet it lacks any visual signature that could have highlighted the protagonist’s internal turmoil or the contrasting personalities of Meera.  

**Music & Background Score** – Composer Vivek Sagar delivers a soundtrack that feels more like filler than a narrative driver. The songs, though pleasant enough, fail to linger in the listener’s mind, and none manage to echo the emotional beats of the story. The background score, in particular, misses opportunities to accentuate the tension when Meera’s personalities shift, leaving those moments feeling flat.  

**Pacing & Editing** – Editor Pradeep E. Ragav keeps the first half brisk, allowing jokes to land before the audience’s attention wanes. However, the second act stretches beyond its natural length, with several scenes lingering without purpose. The uneven pacing contributes to the feeling that the film is trying to stretch a thin premise into a full‑length feature.



### 4. Performances & Characters  

**Priyadarshi (Arjun)** – Known for his impeccable comic timing, Priyadarshi brings a grounded sincerity to the role. He manages to balance slap‑stick moments with a subtle undercurrent of frustration, making his character’s bewilderment believable. Yet, the script offers him limited room to evolve beyond the “confused husband” archetype.  

**Nabha Natesh (Meera)** – Taking on a demanding role that requires rapid emotional pivots, Nabha displays flashes of brilliance, especially in scenes where she switches personas mid‑dialogue. Her willingness to experiment with distinct vocal tones and body language adds texture, though the lack of deeper character development prevents the audience from fully connecting with any single facet of Meera.  

**Brahmanandam** – The legendary comedian appears in a cameo that feels more like a contractual obligation than an organic part of the story. His timing, usually a highlight, is under‑utilised, leaving his scenes undercooked.  

**Vishnu, Krishna Tej, Ananya Nagalla** – The supporting ensemble delivers competent performances but are largely confined to peripheral roles that do not influence the central conflict. Their presence could have enriched the narrative tapestry, yet they remain background fixtures.  

Overall, the cast does its best with the material provided, but the characters themselves suffer from shallow writing, which hampers the actors’ ability to leave a lasting impression.



### 5. Box Office & Collection  

The production kept its budget under wraps, though industry chatter suggests a modestly financed project—typical for a mid‑scale Telugu comedy. As of now, concrete box‑office numbers are unavailable, and early reports indicate a mixed critical reception that could translate into an average theatrical run. The film’s performance will likely hinge on its digital debut; Disney+ Hotstar holds the OTT rights, and a strong streaming viewership could help recoup any shortfall from the theatrical circuit.



### 6. What Works & What Doesn’t  

**Pros**  
- Fresh premise that initially piques curiosity.  
- Priyadarshi’s natural comic flair keeps the first half lively.  
- Nabha Natesh’s willingness to experiment with multiple personas adds occasional depth.  
- Light‑hearted tone suitable for casual weekend viewing.  

**Cons**  
- Narrative loses steam after the midpoint, resulting in a dragging second act.  
- Characters are underwritten, making emotional stakes feel flimsy.  
- Supporting comedians are under‑utilised, squandering potential laughs.  
- Music and background score fail to reinforce mood or highlight personality shifts.  
- Visual style lacks distinctiveness, offering no memorable imagery.  



### 7. My Rating & Final Verdict  

**Rating:** ★★½ / 5  

*Darling* arrives with an intriguing hook but falters in execution. The film offers a few chuckles and showcases the lead actors’ willingness to stretch their abilities, yet it never builds the narrative momentum needed to sustain a full‑length romantic comedy. If you’re looking for a breezy, low‑commitment watch and can overlook the uneven pacing, it might be worth a single sitting. For those seeking a well‑crafted story with resonant humor, the film unfortunately drifts into forgettable territory.
