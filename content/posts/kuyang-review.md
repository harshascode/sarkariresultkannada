## Kuyang Review – When Folklore Meets Fear in Rural Indonesia  



### Movie Summary & Story  

“Kuyang” (2024) is an Indonesian horror venture that dives head‑first into the chilling legend of the eponymous creature—a night‑marish being said to stalk remote villages in search of unborn children. The film follows a young couple, **Rizal** (Dimas Aditya) and his pregnant wife **Sari** (Alyssa Abidin), who relocate to an isolated highland hamlet hoping for a fresh start. Their dream quickly unravels when they discover that the village’s uneasy silence masks an age‑old terror: the Kuyang, a spectral entity that detaches its head, flies on invisible wings, and feeds on the life force of expectant mothers.  

The narrative stakes are immediate and personal. Sari’s pregnancy makes her the perfect bait, turning a simple relocation drama into a race against time. As the couple confronts the villagers’ cryptic warnings, they must decide whether to flee, fight, or sacrifice. The story weaves traditional folklore with contemporary anxieties—urban migration, loss of cultural identity, and the primal fear of losing a child—creating an emotional core that goes beyond cheap jump scares.  

While the full script remains under wraps, the trailer hints at a layered structure: an opening act that establishes the couple’s strained relationship, a middle segment that immerses the audience in the village’s eerie rituals, and a climax that pits love against an ancient, unstoppable hunger. The film promises a slow‑burn tension, where every creaking door and distant whisper feels like a countdown to an inevitable confrontation.

### Technical Aspects  

**Cinematography** – Yongki Ongestu doubles as director and director of photography, a choice that often yields a cohesive visual language. The teaser showcases sweeping aerial shots of mist‑cloaked rice terraces, punctuated by tight, handheld frames inside cramped thatched houses. This contrast amplifies the feeling of isolation while keeping the audience on edge. The muted colour palette—grays, deep greens, and occasional blood‑red accents—mirrors the story’s bleak mood.  

**Music & Sound Design** – Although the composer remains unnamed, the trailer’s ambient drone and intermittent traditional gamelan chimes already hint at a soundscape that leans on regional instruments. The low‑frequency rumble that accompanies the Kuyang’s first appearance is designed to vibrate the theater seats, a classic horror trick that, when paired with sudden silence, heightens the jump‑scare impact.  

**Pacing & Editing** – Early cuts suggest a deliberate, measured pace. The first half spends time establishing the couple’s everyday struggles, allowing viewers to invest emotionally before the horror escalates. The editing appears to favour longer takes during the village rituals, letting tension build organically, before snapping to rapid cuts during the creature’s attacks—an effective rhythm that keeps the audience guessing.  



### Performances & Characters  

**Dimas Aditya (Rizal)** – Known for his work in indie dramas, Aditya brings a grounded, every‑man quality to Rizal. In the teaser, his eyes convey a mixture of optimism and dread, suggesting a character arc that will test his resolve. His physicality—steady yet visibly strained—sets the stage for a believable transformation from reluctant newcomer to desperate protector.  

**Alyssa Abidin (Sari)** – Abidin’s portrayal of a pregnant woman caught in supernatural peril is already resonating with early viewers. Her subtle facial nuances—especially the trembling when she hears the Kuyang’s distant cries—hint at a performance that balances vulnerability with quiet strength. If the script gives her agency, she could become a standout horror heroine, reminiscent of classic Asian cinema’s resilient mothers.  

**Putri Ayudya (Village Elder)** – Though her screen time appears limited, Ayudya’s role as the village’s keeper of lore could serve as the narrative’s moral compass. Her measured delivery in the trailer suggests a character steeped in tradition, whose warnings may either save or doom the protagonists.  

The supporting cast, while not fully disclosed, will need to embody the collective fear and superstition that fuels the film’s atmosphere. Their interactions with the leads will be crucial in grounding the supernatural elements in a believable social fabric.

**Cons**
